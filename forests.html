What Is Deforestation

Deforestation happens when large areas of forest are cleared faster than they can naturally regrow. Trees are removed for farming, building, mining and timber, leaving the land exposed and unable to support the same level of life as before. Once these forests are gone, the soil becomes weak, wildlife loses its habitat and the landscape changes permanently. Although it is a global issue, Ireland has also felt the impact. The country was once almost entirely covered in woodland, yet only a small amount of native forest remains today due to centuries of clearing for agriculture and settlement.

Why Forests Matter

Forests play a vital role in keeping environments stable and healthy. They store carbon and help slow the effects of climate change. They protect soil from erosion, reduce flooding and hold huge amounts of biodiversity. Many species depend on forests for food, shelter and breeding grounds. In Ireland, native woodlands support animals such as red squirrels, pine martens and a wide range of birds and insects. These forests also help manage rainfall, which is becoming more important as storms and heavy rain grow more frequent across the country.

The Main Causes of Forest Loss

Forests are threatened for many reasons. Large areas are cleared for farming, livestock, logging and expanding towns and cities. In other places, rising temperatures and drought lead to wildfires that spread quickly and are difficult to control. While Ireland does not experience large scale tropical deforestation, it faces its own challenges. Native woodland has been reduced over time, and in many areas it has been replaced with fast growing commercial plantations that do not support the same range of species. Storm damage and land use change also contribute to the loss of true native forest.

Key facts about forests in Ireland

Low forest cover

Ireland has much lower forest cover than many other European countries. This makes protecting existing woodland especially important.

Native woodland

Native trees such as oak and birch support a wider mix of plants, insects and animals than single species plantations.

Future planting

New planting plans in Ireland increasingly focus on mixed and native species to improve biodiversity and long term resilience.

Protecting the remaining native woodlands in Ireland is an important step in supporting life on land and strengthening ecosystems for future generations.
